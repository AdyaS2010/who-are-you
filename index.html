The Quest for the True Self

At the heart of WHO//ARE//YOU? lies a single, disquieting question — one philosophy has wrestled with for centuries. This is your personal journey into it. Not a game you win by being fast, but one you win by being honest.

Phase I · The Mask

You build yourself from scratch — traits, interests, a core value, a deepest fear, an aesthetic. The game reveals your Archetype: a philosophical persona mirroring your selections. But is this profile actually you, or a costume you've chosen to wear?

Phase II · The World

You enter a living world and face real moral crossroads. Every choice — about loyalty, silence, rebellion, compassion — invisibly shapes a psychological profile beneath your Mask. The world watches. The world records.

Phase III · The Shift

Your world transforms. Dropped into one of four societies — the Iron Republic, the Unbound, the Collective, the Mirror City — does your identity hold under pressure, or does environment reshape you? Hobbes would argue you never had a choice.

Phase IV · The Glitch

Everything breaks. Your name, your traits, your archetype — stripped away one by one. What remains when the labels dissolve? This phase channels Frank Jackson's haunting question: is there a raw, irreducible you that no description can capture?

Phase V · The Mirror

The game reflects your choices back at you. A full psychological profile emerges — not the self you declared in Phase I, but the self revealed by your decisions. The Mirror doesn't lie. Are the two selves the same?

Phase VI · Trial of Personas

Seven philosophical characters each hold a different answer to "What remains when everything is removed?" You must deduce which one the Philosopher AI is playing — before they deduce you. Choose your school of thought. Choose your opponent. The trial begins.

Gameplay Manual

01 Identity Creation Phase I

  • Name yourself. Enter any name — it echoes through the experience.
  • Choose 3 Traits (e.g. Curious, Resilient, Bold). These seed your psychological alignment.
  • Choose 2 Interests that reflect what genuinely draws you in the world.
  • Select a Core Value — the one principle you refuse to compromise.
  • Name your deepest Fear — what you most want to avoid becoming.
  • Pick an Aesthetic — the emotional tone that feels most like you.
  • Your Archetype is revealed: a philosophical persona derived from your choices. Its backstory is a mirror — read it carefully.

02 The Platformer Journey Phases II–IV

  • Move: ← / → arrow keys or A / D to walk.
  • Jump: Space, W, or ↑. Coyote-time is built in — no unfair falls.
  • Collect choices: Walk into glowing orbs to lock in a decision. Each choice adjusts your alignment vectors.
  • Read the HUD: Scenario text at top; choices at bottom. No timer — take your time and reflect.

03 Trial of Personas Phase VI

  • Choose a Philosophical Role to unlock a one-time strategic ability (Rationalism auto-eliminates 2 suspects; Empiricism reveals the target's core value; Existentialism reveals if they're a nonconformist; Subjectivism senses their aesthetic warmth).
  • Choose a Philosopher Opponent — Hobbes, Hume, Emerson, or Jackson. Different difficulty and deductive strategy.
  • Your turn: Ask a question → answer revealed → eliminate suspects by clicking them → End Turn.
  • AI turn: The Philosopher strategically narrows their list. Watch their suspect count drop in real time.
  • Activate your ability once per game at any point on your turn for a decisive advantage.
  • Final Guess: Click Make Final Guess and select a character card. Beat the AI — or lose the trial.

Philosophy & Sources

Each source shaped a question this game asks. They live inside the characters, the worlds, and every choice you face.

Frank Jackson — Epiphenomenal Qualia 1982

The Philosophical Quarterly, vol. 32, no. 127, pp. 127–136.

Jackson's Mary's Room: if a scientist learns every physical fact about color, does she learn something new when she finally sees red? Yes — suggesting subjective experience (qualia) cannot be reduced to any observable fact. Strip every label away and something deeply personal still remains. This powers Phase IV's stripping sequence and lives in Solara Vale, who believes an essential self persists beneath everything external.

Thomas Hobbes — Leviathan 1651

Project Gutenberg, gutenberg.org/files/3207/3207-h/3207-h.

Without social structure, life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." Identity for Hobbes is molded by environment, fear, and the systems we live under. Remove society's scaffolding and you don't uncover a true self — only a human shaped by survival instinct. Phase III's Iron Republic embodies this directly. Dylan Ward lives the thesis: identity is fully constructed by the forces around us.

Ralph Waldo Emerson — Self-Reliance 1841

Emerson urged radical nonconformity: trust your instincts, resist social pressure, act from your authentic inner voice. Identity is something you choose and assert — not passively discovered. Phase II's crossroads force exactly this tension. Skyler Drew embodies Emerson's creed: "I am what I choose to become in each moment."

Nelson Pike — Hume's Bundle Theory: A Limited Defense 1967

American Philosophical Quarterly, vol. 4, no. 2, pp. 159–165.

Drawing on Hume, Pike examines the claim that no cohesive "self" exists — we are merely a loose collection of shifting experiences. He brings in Buddhist philosophy and Parfit's idea that personal identity is not one stream but a flickering sequence. This drives Riley Flux, whose identity is always in motion — inconsistent, shifting, deliberately uncatchable.

Adrian D. Nelson — Maslow's Matrix 2014

Academia.edu, academia.edu/9447426.

Nelson reframes The Matrix as a story about self-actualization: identity only emerges when you break out of illusion and confront uncomfortable truth. If the world around you is simulated, can your identity be real? This shapes Phase IV's glitch environment and gives life to Axel Mirage, whose paradoxical answers suggest the self is a mental construction the brain tells itself.

Eric T. Olson — Personal Identity 2023

Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, plato.stanford.edu/entries/identity-personal.

Olson maps every major theory of personal identity: psychological continuity, memory theory, bodily continuity, and the no-self view. What makes you the same person over time? Mira Lockwood answers with memory; Alex Mercer answers with labels. The Mirror phase uses Olson's frameworks to reflect your decision-making back with precision.
