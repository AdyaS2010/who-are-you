The Quest for the True Self

In WHO//ARE//YOU?, you begin by constructing a Mask — a collection of social tags, traits, and aesthetics. But is a costume the same as a soul?

As you step into the world, your choices will shape you, shift you under environmental pressures, and ultimately glitch away, stripping you of all labels. You will see your reflection in the Mirror, demonstrating how you navigated the tensions between conformity, independence, logic, and emotion.

Finally, in the Trial of Personas (Guess Who), you must test whether your constructed identity can be deduced using structured reasoning. You will choose a philosophical school of thought and duel against a legendary philosopher. Discover who you are, before they deduce your persona first!

Gameplay Manual

Part 1: The Platformer Journey

  • Movement: Use the ← / → arrows or A / D keys to walk.
  • Jumping: Press Space, W, or ↑ to jump.
  • Objectives: Walk forward to reveal the story nodes. Collect the glowing orbs by walking into them to lock in your choices. Each choice adjusts your psychological alignment.

Part 2: The Trial of Personas (Board Game)

  • Role Selection: Select your philosophical school (e.g., Rationalism, Empiricism) to gain a unique game ability.
  • Opponent Selection: Choose which Philosopher AI you want to challenge (e.g., Thomas Hobbes, David Hume).
  • Turns: On your turn, ask a question from the question bar, then click on suspect cards to manually eliminate them based on the answer.
  • AI Turns: The Philosopher AI will ask questions about your identity and narrow down their board.
  • Victory: Click Make Final Guess when you're confident you know the AI's secret persona card. Guess correctly before the AI guesses yours to win!

Philosophical Sources

Thomas Hobbes — Leviathan (1651)

Hobbes argued that human life in a state of nature is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." To escape this chaos, humans form a Social Contract, sacrificing some freedom for the safety and order of a sovereign state. In the game, Hobbes represents Conformity and Order.

David Hume — A Treatise of Human Nature (1739)

Hume's "Bundle Theory" claims that there is no fixed, permanent "self." Instead, the self is merely a bundle of fleeting sensory perceptions and thoughts in constant motion. In the game, Hume represents Curiosity, Change, and Empiricism.

Ralph Waldo Emerson — Self-Reliance (1841)

Emerson championed nonconformity and listening to one's own inner voice. He urged individuals to trust themselves and resist social pressures to fit in. In the game, Emerson represents Independence and Existentialism.

Frank Jackson — Epiphenomenal Qualia (1982)

Jackson presented the "Mary's Room" thought experiment, arguing that subjective, conscious experiences (qualia) — like the feeling of seeing the color red — cannot be fully explained by physical facts. In the game, Jackson represents Empathy, Subjective Experience, and Qualia.
