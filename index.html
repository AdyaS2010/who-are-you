The Quest for the True Self

WHO//ARE//YOU? explores personal identity. As such, this is not a game you win by being fast, but one you navigate by being honest, with the ultimate goal of revealing something true to your own self.

Phase I : The Mask

You outline your traits, core values, fears, and aesthetic. The game assigns a starting Archetype. Is this profile really you, or just a costume?

Phase II : The World

Face moral decisions in the world. Every action invisibly builds a psychological profile under your mask.

Phase III : The Shift

Your environment changes. Placed in a specialized society, will you adapt and conform, or remain fiercely independent?

Phase IV : The Glitch

The system breaks. Your labels dissolve one by one. What is left of you when your files are corrupted?

Phase V : The Mirror

A direct reflection of your choices. Compare the self you declared with the self revealed by your actions.

Phase VI : Trial of Personas

Eight philosophical concepts each hold a different theory of identity. Deduce the AI's hidden concept before they solve yours.

Gameplay Manual

01 Identity Creation Phase I

  • Name yourself: Enter any name, as it echoes through the experience.
  • Choose 3 Traits: These seed your psychological alignment.
  • Choose 2 Interests: These reflect what genuinely draws you in the world.
  • Select a Core Value: The one principle you refuse to compromise.
  • Name your deepest Fear: What you most want to avoid becoming.
  • Pick an Aesthetic: The emotional tone that feels most like you.
  • Your Archetype is revealed: a philosophical persona derived from your choices. Backstories are mirrors: read them carefully.

02 The Platformer Journey Phases II-IV

  • Move: Use ← / → arrow keys or A / D to walk.
  • Jump: Press Space, W, or ↑. Coyote-time is built in: no unfair falls.
  • Collect choices: Walk into glowing orbs to lock in a decision. Each choice adjusts your alignment vectors.
  • Read the HUD: Scenario text at bottom; choices at left colored buttons.

03 Trial of Personas Phase VI

  • Choose a Philosophical Role: Unlock a one-time strategic ability (Rationalism auto-eliminates 2 suspects; Empiricism reveals the target's core value; Existentialism reveals if they are a nonconformist; Subjectivism senses their aesthetic warmth).
  • Choose a Philosopher Opponent: Challenge Hobbes, Hume, Emerson, or Jackson. Each features a different difficulty and deductive strategy.
  • Your turn: Ask a question, view the answer, and manually eliminate suspects by clicking them. Once done, select End Turn.
  • AI turn: The Philosopher strategically narrows their list. Watch their suspect count drop in real time.
  • Activate your ability: Trigger your role's power once per game at any point on your turn for a decisive advantage.
  • Final Guess: Click Make Final Guess and select a character card. Beat the AI or lose the trial..!

Philosophical Investigation & Conclusions

The Central Question

"Who are you at heart? What remains of the self when all external labels, memories, and social structures are stripped away?"

This project began as an inquiry into the core of human identity. We set out to investigate whether there is an essential, stable "True Self" residing beneath the masks we wear for society, or if identity is a fluid, constructed narrative that dissolves when its components are removed.

Key Findings & Theoretical Tension

Our research explored a dynamic conversation between seven philosophical works, revealing two primary axes of tension:

  • Internal Consciousness vs. Social Construction: Frank Jackson's theory of qualia suggests a private, irreducible subjective experience at the heart of identity ("what it is like to be you"), which Ralph Waldo Emerson extends into the moral domain of absolute self-reliance. Conversely, Thomas Hobbes' Leviathan and Adrian D. Nelson's analysis of The Matrix argue that identity is fundamentally constructed by external forces, whether they be social contracts born of fear or simulated realities that dictate behavior.
  • Continuity vs. Flux: Eric T. Olson highlights the traditional frameworks of memory and bodily continuity as the anchors of persistence over time. However, Nelson Pike's defense of Hume's Bundle Theory and Buddhist concepts of Anatta (No-Self) destabilize this view, proposing that the self is not a single, continuous entity but a shifting bundle of transient perceptions with no permanent core.

Our Final Conclusions

Based on our investigation, we conclude that identity is not a static object to be discovered, but an active, ongoing process of self-authoring. When all external labels, memory chains, and social roles are stripped away, what remains is not a blank void, nor is it a rigid, pre-written soul. Rather, it is the raw capacity for subjective awareness (Jackson's qualia) and the agency to choose (Emerson's self-reliance).

As demonstrated in the game's final Mirror phase, you are neither solely the labels you define for yourself in Phase I, nor are you merely a passive product of the environments you traverse in Phase III. The "True Self" is the active observer and creator, the conscious agent that navigates the tension between the individual and the collective, memory and change. You are the weaver of your own shifting experiences.

Philosophy & Sources

Each of these philosophical works helped build this game. They live inside the characters, the worlds, and the choices you make as you explore who you are.

Ralph Waldo Emerson: Self-Reliance 1841

Essays: First Series, 1841.

Emerson’s argument in Self-Reliance is that instead of conforming to societal norms, we should rely on ourselves. He famously wrote, "Nothing is at last sacred but the integrity of your own mind" and urged, "Trust thyself: every heart vibrates to that iron string." This source, when used for our particular question, provides the perspective that "we are who we believe": we can be anything, and the real "self" emerges once one stops performing for the world and starts trusting their own intuition. Emerson argues that "whoso would be a man, must be a nonconformist," meaning that it is imperative to stop being what others expect in order to find the "you" underneath all the labels. As such, Self-Reliance provides a hopeful, almost empowering angle that the core self isn’t lost, but just waiting to be realized. This work directly inspires The Independent, who trusts their own path above all else. His perspective balances Hobbes’ well, reminding us that identity can be formed internally with self-consciousness, not only as imposed by society.

Thomas Hobbes: Leviathan 1651

Gutenberg.org, 1651, www.gutenberg.org/files/3207/3207-h/3207-h.

In Leviathan, Hobbes argues that humans are fundamentally shaped by fear, the preservation of oneself, and the need for order. In the state of nature, he asserts that "the life of man [is] solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short," leading humans to seek shelter under a sovereign authority because "covenants, without the sword, are but words and of no strength to secure a man at all." His view thereby implies that identity is not something that is of a fixed nature or inherent, but instead molded by our environment and social structures. Additionally, this perspective challenges the idea of a stable inner self and suggests that who we are is largely determined as a product of our environment and our own survival instincts. Hobbes raises the possibility that once you remove labels and roles, you may not uncover a "true self" at all, but only a human being shaped by fear and the immense desire for security. This concept directly defines The Citizen, who relies on the social contract for order and survival.

Frank Jackson: Epiphenomenal Qualia 1982

The Philosophical Quarterly, vol. 32, no. 127, Apr. 1982, pp. 127-136, https://doi.org/10.2307/2960077.

“Epiphenomenal Qualia” by Frank Jackson offers insight into ‘qualia’: the unique, private, and subjective “feels” of experiences. He observes that "it seems that the qualitative character of our experiences is not captured by physicalist descriptions," arguing that a scientist (like his famous thought experiment character Mary) could know every physical fact about the world but still learn something new when experiencing a color for the first time because "nothing you could tell of a physical sort... will capture the subjective feel of experience." This view suggests that once stripped of all external labels, roles, possessions, and physical characteristics, there is still something deeply personal to each person that remains: "there is something it is like to be a conscious being, something it is like for the being itself." Thus, individuality is rooted in private consciousness. This idea manifests in The Experiencer, who holds that the raw "what it is like" to exist remains even when everything else is removed.

Adrian D. Nelson: Maslow’s Matrix: Depictions of Self-Actualization in the Motion Picture, The Matrix 2014

Academia.edu, 22 Nov. 2014, www.academia.edu/9447426/Maslows_Matrix_Depictions_of_Self_actualization_in_the_Motion_Picture_The_Matrix.

Nelson’s analysis of The Matrix through Maslow’s “hierarchy of needs” reframes the film as a story regarding self-actualization, which consists of breaking out of an illusion to discover who you truly are. He writes that "The Matrix serves as an allegory for self-actualization," and that "Neo’s choice to unplug from the simulated reality represents the painful but necessary journey towards self-actualization." This is incredibly relevant to our concept, as the movie directly confronts the debate whether identity can be considered 'real' if the world around you is fake or manipulated. The paper helps us think about identity as something that can be distorted by the environment, supporting similar views as expressed in Hobbes’ Leviathan, and something that may emerge only when one confronts uncomfortable truths. This aligns with The Dreamer, who views identity as a simulated projection that must be shattered to see what is real.

Eric T. Olson: Personal Identity (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy) 2023

Stanford.edu, 30 June 2023, plato.stanford.edu/entries/identity-personal/.

This Stanford article provides a comprehensive overview of theories of personal identity, including those like psychological as well as bodily continuity, memory theory, and the no-self view. Olson writes that "the question of personal identity is the question of what it takes for a past or future person to be you," outlining the major frameworks philosophers use to understand what makes someone considerably the "same person" over time. Overall, the source serves as a structured manner in which we may analyze our question and evaluate the many competing and compelling answers as to what remains when everything external to one’s identity is taken away. These frameworks form the basis of both The Human (defined by labels and biological existence) and The Chronicler (defined by memory continuity).

Nelson Pike: Hume’s Bundle Theory of the Self: A Limited Defense 1967

American Philosophical Quarterly, vol. 4, no. 2, 1967, pp. 159-165, www.jstor.org/stable/20009239.

In this article, Pike examines Hume’s “Bundle Theory of Self,” which essentially argues that no cohesive 'self' exists. Hume famously asserted: "I may venture to affirm of the rest of mankind, that they are nothing but a bundle or collection of different perceptions, which succeed each other with an inconceivable rapidity, and are in a perpetual flux and movement." Pike further expands on this concept by bringing in Buddhism and Parfit's idea of self, aligning with Bundle Theory in that there is no "self" hiding under anything, as the bundle actually is the self. This article touches on the idea that we don’t have a single stream of consciousness, suggesting that once you remove yourself from your associations, there might be nothing left, not because the self is 'hidden', but since it was never a singular thing to begin with. This directly underpins The Bundle (defined by constant sensory flux) and The Illusion (the Buddhist realization that no permanent self exists).

Neil Roughley: Human Nature 2021

Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Metaphysics Research Lab, Stanford University, 15 Mar. 2021, plato.stanford.edu/entries/human-nature/.

Roughley’s entry “Human Nature” in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy explains that there is no single agreed-upon definition of humanity, but instead multiple varying views. He writes that "any account of human nature must address both the biological boundaries and the cultural malleability of our species," examining how the same concept is utilized in debates about subjects familiar to us, such as ethics, evolution, and identity. All in all, this source is useful in answering our question because it breaks down such a complex, perhaps vague concept into many different theories, and shows why the question of humanity and identity is established as one of the most prominent debates in philosophy today. This foundational inquiry is embodied by The Human, who builds their identity out of human nature, social roles, and relationships.
