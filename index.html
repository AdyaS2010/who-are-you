The Quest for the True Self

At the heart of WHO//ARE//YOU? lies a single, disquieting question: one that philosophy has wrestled with for centuries. This is your personal journey into it. Not a game you win by being fast, but one you win by being honest.

Phase I : The Mask

You build yourself from scratch: traits, interests, a core value, a deepest fear, and an aesthetic. The game reveals your Archetype: a philosophical persona mirroring your selections. But is this profile actually you, or a costume you have chosen to wear?

Phase II : The World

You enter a living world and face real moral crossroads. Every choice, about loyalty, silence, rebellion, or compassion, invisibly shapes a psychological profile beneath your Mask. The world is watching and recording.

Phase III : The Shift

Your world transforms. Dropped into one of four societies (the Iron Republic, the Unbound, the Collective, or the Mirror City), does your identity hold under pressure, or does the environment reshape you? Hobbes would argue you never had a choice.

Phase IV : The Glitch

Everything breaks. Your name, your traits, your archetype: stripped away one by one. What remains when the labels dissolve? This phase channels Frank Jackson's haunting question: is there a raw, irreducible self that no description can capture?

Phase V : The Mirror

The game reflects your choices back at you. A full psychological profile emerges: not the self you declared in Phase I, but the self revealed by your decisions. The Mirror does not lie. Are the two selves the same?

Phase VI : Trial of Personas

Seven philosophical characters each hold a different answer to the question: "What remains when everything is removed?" You must deduce which one the Philosopher AI is playing, before they deduce you. Choose your school of thought and your opponent. The trial begins.

Gameplay Manual

01 Identity Creation Phase I

  • Name yourself: Enter any name, as it echoes through the experience.
  • Choose 3 Traits: These seed your psychological alignment.
  • Choose 2 Interests: These reflect what genuinely draws you in the world.
  • Select a Core Value: The one principle you refuse to compromise.
  • Name your deepest Fear: What you most want to avoid becoming.
  • Pick an Aesthetic: The emotional tone that feels most like you.
  • Your Archetype is revealed: a philosophical persona derived from your choices. Its backstory is a mirror: read it carefully.

02 The Platformer Journey Phases II-IV

  • Move: Use ← / → arrow keys or A / D to walk.
  • Jump: Press Space, W, or ↑. Coyote-time is built in: no unfair falls.
  • Collect choices: Walk into glowing orbs to lock in a decision. Each choice adjusts your alignment vectors.
  • Read the HUD: Scenario text at top; choices at bottom. No timer: take your time and reflect.

03 Trial of Personas Phase VI

  • Choose a Philosophical Role: Unlock a one-time strategic ability (Rationalism auto-eliminates 2 suspects; Empiricism reveals the target's core value; Existentialism reveals if they are a nonconformist; Subjectivism senses their aesthetic warmth).
  • Choose a Philosopher Opponent: Challenge Hobbes, Hume, Emerson, or Jackson. Each features a different difficulty and deductive strategy.
  • Your turn: Ask a question, view the answer, and manually eliminate suspects by clicking them. Once done, select End Turn.
  • AI turn: The Philosopher strategically narrows their list. Watch their suspect count drop in real time.
  • Activate your ability: Trigger your role's power once per game at any point on your turn for a decisive advantage.
  • Final Guess: Click Make Final Guess and select a character card. Beat the AI or lose the trial.

Philosophy & Sources

Each of these philosophical works helped build this game. They live inside the characters, the worlds, and the choices you make as you explore who you are.

Ralph Waldo Emerson: Self-Reliance 1841

Essays: First Series

Emerson calls for radical self-trust: do not follow society's expectations, and instead listen to your own inner voice. In the game, this shapes Skyler Drew, who believes you are not something to be found, but something you actively create through your choices.

Thomas Hobbes: Leviathan 1651

Project Gutenberg, gutenberg.org/files/3207/3207-h/3207-h

Hobbes argued that human life without structure is solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short. Our identity is molded by rules and the need for security. This powers the Iron Republic in Phase III and defines Dylan Ward, who believes identity is manufactured by society.

Frank Jackson: Epiphenomenal Qualia 1982

The Philosophical Quarterly, vol. 32, no. 127, pp. 127-136

Through the Mary's Room thought experiment, Jackson shows that subjective, personal experiences (qualia) cannot be reduced to physical facts. This shapes Phase IV's glitch sequence and defines Solara Vale, who holds that a raw, private core remains even when all labels are gone.

Adrian D. Nelson: Maslow's Matrix 2014

Academia.edu, academia.edu/9447426

Nelson connects self-actualization with breaking out of simulated illusions. If our environment is built on facades, is the self real? This inspires the glitch environment in Phase IV and shapes Axel Mirage, who views the self as a mental illusion.

Eric T. Olson: Personal Identity 2023

Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, plato.stanford.edu/entries/identity-personal

Olson details how we stay the same person over time. Does continuity come from memory, labels, or the physical body? This fuels the Mirror in Phase V and guides Mira Lockwood (memory self) and Alex Mercer (label self).

Nelson Pike: Hume's Bundle Theory: A Limited Defense 1967

American Philosophical Quarterly, vol. 4, no. 2, pp. 159-165

Pike defends Hume's view that the self is not a single, permanent entity, but a loose bundle of changing perceptions in constant motion. This is the foundation of Riley Flux, whose identity shifts constantly like a stream.

Neil Roughley: Human Nature 2021

Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, plato.stanford.edu/entries/human-nature/

Roughley explores what qualities make us fundamentally human. Is there a fixed human essence, or are we entirely molded by culture and environment? This tension drives Phase III's world shifts, where environmental pressures test your starting nature.
