The Quest for the True Self

WHO//ARE//YOU? explores personal identity. As such, this is not a game you win by being fast, but one you navigate by being honest, with the ultimate goal of revealing something true to your own self.

Phase I : The Mask

You outline your traits, core values, fears, and aesthetic. The game assigns a starting Archetype. Is this profile really you, or just a costume?

Phase II : The World

Face moral decisions in the world. Every action invisibly builds a psychological profile under your mask.

Phase III : The Shift

Your environment changes. Placed in a specialized society, will you adapt and conform, or remain fiercely independent?

Phase IV : The Glitch

The system breaks. Your labels dissolve one by one. What is left of you when your files are corrupted?

Phase V : The Mirror

A direct reflection of your choices. Compare the self you declared with the self revealed by your actions.

Phase VI : Trial of Personas

Eight philosophical concepts each hold a different theory of identity. Deduce the AI's hidden concept before they solve yours.

Gameplay Manual

01 Identity Creation Phase I

  • Name yourself: Enter any name, as it echoes through the experience.
  • Choose 3 Traits: These seed your psychological alignment.
  • Choose 2 Interests: These reflect what genuinely draws you in the world.
  • Select a Core Value: The one principle you refuse to compromise.
  • Name your deepest Fear: What you most want to avoid becoming.
  • Pick an Aesthetic: The emotional tone that feels most like you.
  • Your Archetype is revealed: a philosophical persona derived from your choices. Its backstory is a mirror: read it carefully.

02 The Platformer Journey Phases II-IV

  • Move: Use ← / → arrow keys or A / D to walk.
  • Jump: Press Space, W, or ↑. Coyote-time is built in: no unfair falls.
  • Collect choices: Walk into glowing orbs to lock in a decision. Each choice adjusts your alignment vectors.
  • Read the HUD: Scenario text at bottom; choices at left colored buttons.

03 Trial of Personas Phase VI

  • Choose a Philosophical Role: Unlock a one-time strategic ability (Rationalism auto-eliminates 2 suspects; Empiricism reveals the target's core value; Existentialism reveals if they are a nonconformist; Subjectivism senses their aesthetic warmth).
  • Choose a Philosopher Opponent: Challenge Hobbes, Hume, Emerson, or Jackson. Each features a different difficulty and deductive strategy.
  • Your turn: Ask a question, view the answer, and manually eliminate suspects by clicking them. Once done, select End Turn.
  • AI turn: The Philosopher strategically narrows their list. Watch their suspect count drop in real time.
  • Activate your ability: Trigger your role's power once per game at any point on your turn for a decisive advantage.
  • Final Guess: Click Make Final Guess and select a character card. Beat the AI or lose the trial..!

Philosophy & Sources

Each of these philosophical works helped build this game. They live inside the characters, the worlds, and the choices you make as you explore who you are.

Ralph Waldo Emerson: Self-Reliance 1841

Essays: First Series, 1841.

Emerson’s argument in Self-Reliance is that instead of conforming to societal norms, we should rely on ourselves. This source, when used for our particular question, provides the perspective that “we are who we believe”: we can be anything, and the real “self” emerges once one stops performing for the world and starts trusting their own intuition. This source really explores who we can be as unique individuals if we don’t constantly conform to society and attempt to adopt identities that aren’t completely ours. Emerson urges people to be authentic, meaning that it is imperative that you stop being what others expect in order to find the “you” underneath all the labels, which does exist. As such, Self-Reliance provides a hopeful, almost empowering angle that the core self isn’t lost, but just waiting to be realized. This work directly inspires ***The Independent***, who trusts their own path above all else. His perspective balances Hobbes’ well, reminding us that identity can be formed internally with self-consciousness, not only as imposed by society.

Thomas Hobbes: Leviathan 1651

Gutenberg.org, 1651, www.gutenberg.org/files/3207/3207-h/3207-h.

In Leviathan, Hobbes argues that humans are fundamentally shaped by fear, the preservation of oneself, and the need for order in life and society. His view thereby implies that identity is not something that is of a fixed nature or inherent, but instead molded by our environment and social structures and pressures we live under. Additionally, this perspective challenges the idea of a stable inner self and suggests that who we are is largely determined as a product of our environment and our own survival instincts. In relation to our questions, Hobbes raises the possibility that once you remove labels and roles, you may not uncover a “true self” at all, but only a human being shaped by fear and the immense desire for security, conveying the extent to which identity is constructed by society rather than internal discovery for the most part. This concept directly defines ***The Citizen***, who relies on the social contract for order and survival.

Frank Jackson: Epiphenomenal Qualia 1982

The Philosophical Quarterly, vol. 32, no. 127, Apr. 1982, pp. 127-136, https://doi.org/10.2307/2960077.

“Epiphenomenal Qualia” by Frank Jackson offers insight into ‘qualia’: the unique, private, and subjective “feels” of experiences, providing one of the most intimate perspectives regarding identity. He essentially says that the inner sensations experienced by oneself can’t be reduced to comprehensible physical facts. This view proves incredibly plausible in answering our question: “Who are you at heart?”, as it suggests that once stripped of all external labels, roles, possessions, physical characteristics, etc., there is still something deeply personal to each person that remains. Through this article, one is able to come to the conclusion that the “self” may not be found in anything observable, but rather in the raw, natural texture of experiences unique to an individual. Thus, we may explore whether a core “you” actually exists beneath everything else, and whether individuality is in fact rooted in consciousness instead of simple identity markers. This idea manifests in ***The Experiencer***, who holds that the raw "what it is like" to exist remains even when everything else is removed.

Adrian D. Nelson: Maslow’s Matrix: Depictions of Self-Actualization in the Motion Picture, The Matrix 2014

Academia.edu, 22 Nov. 2014, www.academia.edu/9447426/Maslows_Matrix_Depictions_of_Self_actualization_in_the_Motion_Picture_The_Matrix.

Nelson’s analysis of The Matrix through Maslow’s “hierarchy of needs”, the inspiration to the simulated reality observed in The Matrix, reframes the film as a story regarding ‘self-actualization’, which consists of breaking out of an illusion to discover who you truly are. This is incredibly relevant to our concept of study, as the movie directly confronts the debate whether identity can be considered ‘real’ if the world around you is fake or manipulated. The paper helps us think about identity as something that can be distorted by the environment, supporting similar views as expressed in Hobbes’ Leviathan, and something that may emerge only when one confronts uncomfortable truths. His perspective also adds a psychological and cinematic dimension to our topic, proposing that maybe the “self” is something you grow into once you stop accepting the world at face value. This aligns with ***The Dreamer***, who views identity as a simulated projection that must be shattered to see what is real.

Eric T. Olson: Personal Identity (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy) 2023

Stanford.edu, 30 June 2023, plato.stanford.edu/entries/identity-personal/.

This Stanford article provides a comprehensive overview of theories of personal identity, including those like psychological as well as bodily continuity, memory theory, and the no-self view. Olson’s discussion helps us examine whether identity is rooted in memory, consciousness, physical form, or something else entirely, and proposes ideas regarding what happens to the “self” when these components are removed, thus laying out the major frameworks philosophers use to understand what makes someone considerably the “same person” over time. Overall, the source serves as a structured manner in which we may analyze our question and evaluate the many competing and compelling answers as to what remains when everything external to one’s identity is taken away. These frameworks form the basis of both ***The Human*** (defined by labels and biological existence) and ***The Chronicler*** (defined by memory continuity).

Nelson Pike: Hume’s Bundle Theory of the Self: A Limited Defense 1967

American Philosophical Quarterly, vol. 4, no. 2, 1967, pp. 159-165, www.jstor.org/stable/20009239.

In this article, Pike examines Hume’s “Bundle Theory of Self”, which essentially argues that no cohesive ‘self’ exists. Instead, humans are different only by loosely connected and constantly shifting individual perceptions and experiences that happen to us: we are just properties, and without them, there is nothing to define. Pike then further expands on this concept by bringing in Buddhism and philosopher Parfit’s idea of self, which aligns with Hume’s Bundle Theory in that there is, in fact, no “self” hiding under anything, as the bundle actually is the self. This article touches on the idea proposed by Parfit that we don’t have a single stream of consciousness, and provides one of the most radical answers to our question, as it suggests that indeed, once you remove yourself from your associations, there might be nothing left, and not because the self is ‘hidden’, per se, but since it was never a singular thing to begin with; it adds a destabilizing perspective that identity is similar to gas particles in a sense, fluid, unstable, and always in motion. This directly underpins ***The Bundle*** (defined by constant sensory flux) and ***The Illusion*** (the Buddhist realization that no permanent self exists).

Neil Roughley: Human Nature 2021

Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Metaphysics Research Lab, Stanford University, 15 Mar. 2021, plato.stanford.edu/entries/human-nature/.

Roughley’s entry “Human Nature” in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy provides a detailed philosophical overview of what “human nature” means and how different thinkers have interpreted it. The article explains that there is no single agreed-upon definition of humanity, but instead multiple varying views, known as psychological and philosophical understandings. It is also discussed how some philosophers see human nature as a set of traits shared by all humans, while others may argue that human beings are shaped by the culture and environment around them, along with their own individual development. Roughley also examines how the same concept is utilized in debates about subjects familiar to us, such as ethics, evolution, and identity. All in all, this source is useful in answering our question because it breaks down such a complex, perhaps vague concept into many different theories, and shows why the question of humanity and individuality is still established as one of the most prominent debates in philosophy today. This foundational inquiry is embodied by ***The Human***, who builds their identity out of human nature, social roles, and relationships.
